Discussed CTA Colour. Go with Option B.

The current yellow buttons are hard to read on cream and pink backgrounds — there isn't enough contrast between the button and the surface behind it. Below are two alternative systems, each pairing a CTA treatment with an offer/sale treatment. Both use Oat Cult Blue for offer badges.

CTA rationale: Brown on cream and pink is clearly legible on every background (passes WCAG AA — 12.82:1 and 5.28:1). On dark sections the pair inverts: cream on brown. Trade-off: Brown/cream inverse — same colour pair, just flipped on dark backgrounds. The rule is as simple as it gets. Blue badges lean on brand identity over urgency convention.

Discussed Imagery Direction.

An audit of current imagery across the site, followed by a proposed direction for how the two layers — graphic illustration and real photography — could work together as a system.

Summary: 3 assets to replace, 3 to keep, 7 strong references. The proposed direction: a two-layer system — graphic illustration for brand moments, real photography for product moments.

Brand & Lifestyle Imagery.

Assessment of current brand and lifestyle imagery across the site.

B&W Popup (Man with Bowl)
Replace

Tonal mismatch — reads as hardship, not "bold flavour overnight oats." Dark, grainy B&W next to "Join the Cult" creates uncomfortable association. No graphic layer, no palette colours. Breaks the system entirely. Actively hurts popup conversion rate.

Figure (Oat Head, Blue Geometric Bg)
Replace

Visually arresting but disconnected from product. Abstract editorial that says "art gallery" not "breakfast." The blue isn't the brand blue. First-time visitor learns nothing about the product in 2–3 seconds. Doesn't use the monk illustration system.

Illustrated Popup (Graphic Oaty Udders)
Strong

Product present, brand palette present (pink/brown/cream), "cult" personality comes through. Ownable — no other oats brand looks like this. Works at popup scale. The benchmark for graphic-led brand imagery.

Product Imagery.

Assessment of current product photography across the site.

Raw Oats Pour (Blue-Grey Bowl)
Replace

Off-brand colours (grey marble, blue-grey bowl, pink tea towel — none are palette colours). Shows dry oats being poured — the least appetising moment. No packaging, no brand presence, no graphic layer. Can't be rescued with a monk overlay; needs reshooting on-palette.

Sachet-in-Hand (Pink Graphic Background)
Keep

Product-in-hand, bold saturated brand-colour background, close crop, packaging clearly readable. The fist-grip has confident, rebellious energy. The hybrid approach (real photo composited on graphic bg) bridges illustration and product photography perfectly.

Product Lineup on Pink (PDP Gallery)
Keep

Shows full range clearly (Strawberry Goji/Cinnamon/Cacao), on-palette pink background, good for product grid. Pink sachets blend into pink bg slightly. Best as PDP image 2–3, not the lead image.

Proposed Direction.

A two-layer imagery system is already emerging in the strongest assets above. Below are examples — mostly from existing site content — that show how graphic-led brand imagery and photo-led product imagery could work together as a deliberate system. The illustrated popup assessed above is the benchmark for the graphic-led side; the examples here build on that foundation. This is a recommendation, not a locked decision.

How-to-Prepare Monks (Brown Bg)
Strong

Pure illustration in the right context — step-by-step instructions benefit from it because you're teaching, not selling. Pink circles on brown is on-palette. The monk's hands interacting with real-looking product (sachet, jar, bowl) keeps it grounded. Works as homepage "how it works" strip or PDP section.

Sachet Fan with Monk (Pink Bg, Probiotic)
Strong

Real sachets are the hero — three flavours fanned, packaging readable, brand name prominent. Small arms-raised monk at bottom-right adds personality without competing. Faded probiotic graphic at top-right layers in the gut health messaging subtly. Benefit bullets scannable and well-spaced. Headline copy ("gut-worshipping live cultures") lands the product story with brand voice. Two levels of graphic — solid illustration and ghosted accent — working at different intensities on one card.

Review Speech Bubbles (Cream/Pink/Blue)
Keep

Pure social proof in brand-colour speech bubbles — pink, blue, cream all on-palette. Real customer voice matches brand personality. Functional and effective. Could be elevated further with a small monk or product element alongside, but works well as-is for testimonial sections.

Review Card (Cacao Bowl, Pink Bg, Oaty Udder)
Strong — Benchmark

The finished cacao bowl is the hero — real food, appetising, topped generously. Oaty udder graphic in the corner adds brand personality without competing with the product. Real customer review underneath builds trust. Pink background on-palette. Photo carries appetite appeal, illustration carries identity, review carries social proof — three jobs, one card. This is the benchmark for photo-led product imagery.

Benefits Section (Three Bowls, Monk, Light Blue Bg)
Strong

Three real bowls with fresh toppings (strawberry, banana, blueberry) are the clear hero — overhead angle is appetising, each flavour distinct. Benefit callouts sit above and below the bowls in a clean grid — six claims scannable at a glance. Small arms-raised monk at the bottom adds personality without competing. Light blue background on-palette, calmer than bold blue which lets the food colours pop. The graphic-to-product ratio is well judged here — product dominates, illustration supports.

Back-of-Pack (Brown Sachet, Light Blue Bg, Probiotic)
Strong

Full back-of-pack on a light blue background — nutritional info, prep steps, and credentials all readable. Pink probiotic graphic peeking from the top-right corner adds brand personality to what could be a dry information shot. Probiotic tagline banner across the top ties into the gut health messaging. Brown sachet on blue is on-palette. Shows the packaging illustration system (how-to icons, credential badges) carries through to the physical product. For web: PDP "what's inside" or ingredients sections.

The opportunity: a two-layer imagery system is already emerging in the strongest assets. We'd recommend leaning into it as the defining visual identity — graphic illustration and real photography working together, with clear rules for when each layer leads.

The split: if the goal is "feel the brand" → graphic-led (hero banners, popups, storytelling, social). If the goal is "see/buy the product" → photo-led with graphic accent (PDP, product grid, reviews, benefits).

Why it works: real photos alone look generic (any oat brand). Illustration alone doesn't sell the food. Together, the monk system gives Oat Cult something ownable — no competitor has this — while real product photography builds the trust and appetite appeal that illustrations can't.

What to retire: assets that have neither layer — the B&W popup, raw oats pour, and abstract figure. These break the system because they have no illustration, no on-palette treatment, and can't be fixed with a monk overlay. They need replacing, not patching.

The missing shoot list: new product photography should be shot for graphic compositing — clean edges, on-palette backgrounds, space for the monk or graphic elements to be layered in.

  1. Finished bowls — overhead, on pink/brown/cream backgrounds, with toppings. One per flavour.
  2. Morning ritual — sachet torn open, milk poured, jar going into fridge. Real hands, brand-colour surfaces.
  3. Lifestyle context — someone eating at a table/desk/on a commute. Real person, warm light, on-brand colours.

Reference brands: Innocent Drinks (real product + illustrated character system), Oatly, Liquid Death — all illustration-led, among the most shared DTC brands.

Pushing the Envelope.

An alternative direction that takes the hybrid approach further — fusing real product photography directly with illustration rather than keeping them as separate layers.

Photo-Illustration Composite (Monk Hand, Real Bowl — Pink Bg)
Strong

Real food (textured oats, fresh blueberries) composited onto an illustrated monk hand. The food carries the trust and appetite job, the hand carries the identity job — instantly recognisable as Oat Cult even without a logo. Bold pink background makes this the hero variant — social, ads, homepage banners.

Photo-Illustration Composite (Monk Hand, Real Bowl — Cream Bg)
Strong

Same composite technique on a calmer cream background. Less scroll-stopping than the pink variant but more versatile — works for PDP hero images, product grid, and contexts where the food needs to sell without competing with the background. The pair together gives flexibility across every touchpoint.

Hero Pack (Monk Hands, Cacao Sachet — Pink/Cream Bg)
Strong

Illustrated blue monk fists gripping a real cacao sachet — the composite approach applied to packaging rather than bowls. Packaging readable, brand name prominent, flavour clear. Bold pink and cream lightning shapes behind add energy. Proves the monk hand system scales beyond food to product-in-hand moments. The fist grip matches the rebellious energy of the sachet-in-hand photo from the audit, but now fully ownable. Hero, social, and ad use.

What this changes: the proposed split above keeps graphic and photo as two distinct layers. This approach merges them into a single image — real product held by illustrated hands, on bold palette backgrounds. It's a tighter, more distinctive system where every product image is unmistakably Oat Cult.

The upside: product imagery becomes as ownable as the brand imagery. No competitor could use the same shots. Scroll-stopping on social. The monk hand becomes a visual signature across every touchpoint.

What to consider: compositing quality needs to be consistent — the join between real bowl and illustrated hand is where it lives or dies. This works well for "held bowl" shots but may be harder for pouring moments or sachet-in-hand where the interaction is more complex. Needs a clear production process to maintain quality at scale.

If you go this route: shoot product photography on neutral backgrounds with clean edges specifically for compositing. One illustrated hand template per pose, then swap in different bowls/sachets/products. Pink bg for hero and social, cream for PDP, brown for premium moments.

Conversion Principles.

Brand-level principles for how conversion tactics should feel within Oat Cult.

CTA Presence.
Confident and visible — the CTA pops without shouting. Every scroll position on mobile should have a clear next action within thumb reach.
Urgency.
Earned, not manufactured — "cult favourite" and social proof over countdown timers or fake scarcity.
Trust Signals.
Woven into the product story — "1 billion live cultures", certifications alongside ingredients, not in a bolted-on badge strip.
Subscription.
Sub-first model. Present subscription as the default highlighted option. Frame the saving as community — "Join the Cult and save 20%" — not just a discount.
Cart & Checkout.
Frictionless and warm. Brown and cream keep it calm, the CTA stays prominent. Brand personality carries through to payment.
Mobile.
Thumb-first — key actions in bottom half of viewport, generous tap targets (min 44px), no pinch-to-zoom on product images.

Body: Inter (Google Fonts) — paragraphs, meta, descriptions

Logo: Blackletter — display only, never for web text

Mobile Experience.

75% of traffic is mobile — mobile is the primary experience, desktop is the adaptation.

Touch Targets.
Generous, never cramped. Minimum 44px tap targets on all interactive elements. Spacing between tappable items prevents mis-taps.
Thumb Zone.
Primary actions reachable without stretching. Key CTAs live in the bottom half of the viewport — the natural thumb arc.
Typography.
Body text never shrinks below 16px. Headings scale per the type table — H1 drops from 56px to 36px, H2 from 42px to 28px. Readability is non-negotiable.
Spacing.
Tighter section padding on mobile (48px vs 80px desktop), but never cramped — content breathes. Card padding stays at 24px.
Imagery.
Full-width, immersive, no zooming required. Product images sized so detail is clear at phone width without pinch-to-zoom.
